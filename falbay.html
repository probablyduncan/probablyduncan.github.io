Falmouth Bay:
Abstracts in a
Foreign Land


Photographed and Written
Autumn 2019



W

hen I landed in Cornwall, 6,000 kilometres from home, I felt, as one might expect, out of place. My home and my friends and lake were behind me, and naught but the towering mountain of University laid ahead.

For weeks, I resisted missing home. I was happy in Cornwall and didn't regret a thing, and I was afraid that missing home would be ungrateful to all the opportunities that landed me in Cornwall.

A gull, wraithlike, careens across the cloudy dawn.

The pastels of sunset glow in the sand and eastern sky.

A cormorant skims the sea just after sunrise.

These three photos of the same water are progressively more detailed.

These two photos show the same horizon a minute and a half apart: the first as the sun broke the horizon, and the second as the sun rose above the frame.

I've been thinking a lot about change recently

I took these two abstracts from the same place in the hour before sunset. The first looks south, to still-bright water, and the second looks east, towards the dimming horizon..

What I've realized is that change is additive: it can't erase the person I once was, only add to it. The many facets of me are not exclusive, and I don't lose one identity by gaining another.

I know now that happiness for the new and longing for the old are not exclusive. I can be simultaneously grateful for my presence here in Cornwall and wistful for home. And as trivial or obvious as this may seem, it's incredibly liberating.
